Section 01

The Tools You Need

Before you touch a wall, gather these. Most are cheap and will last a lifetime. Don't skip the stud finder or spirit level — they prevent the most common beginner mistakes.

  • 🔨 Hammer
  • 🔩 Electric drill/driver
  • 🔦 Stud finder
  • 📏 Spirit level (even a small one)
  • 📐 Tape measure
  • ✏️ Pencil
  • 🖇️ Masking tape
  • 🧲 Magnet (optional, for pipes/cables)
⚠ Before You Drill Anything

Use a pipe and cable detector (about £15–25 at Screwfix or B&Q). Drilling into a hidden electric cable or water pipe is genuinely dangerous and expensive to fix. Always scan the area first. Cables typically run vertically from sockets/switches and horizontally at set heights.

Section 02

Know Your Wall Type

This is the most important thing to get right. The wrong fixing for the wrong wall is how things fall off — or worse, pull a chunk of plaster with them. Here's how to identify what you're dealing with.

🧱

Solid Brick or Block

Very common in UK homes, especially external walls and pre-1970s builds. Feels solid when knocked. Drill feels resistance all the way through. The strongest type — can hold almost anything.

🪵

Dot & Dab Plasterboard

Modern UK standard. Plasterboard fixed to a masonry wall using blobs of adhesive. Sounds hollow in spots, solid in others. Tricky — you need to hit the solid blobs or use specialist anchors.

🗂️

Stud Wall (Timber Frame)

Internal partition walls. Plasterboard fixed to a timber frame. Completely hollow between studs (~400–600mm apart). Sounds hollow throughout. Screw into studs for strength.

🏗️

Metal Stud (Modern Offices/Flats)

Like timber stud but with metal C-section uprights. Common in newer builds and commercial-to-residential conversions. Use plasterboard anchors or self-drilling fixings.

🏛️

Lath & Plaster (Old Homes)

Pre-1950s. Thin wooden strips (laths) covered with several coats of plaster. Fragile. Drill slowly, don't hammer. Use the lath strips or find a stud behind.

🪨

Concrete / Dense Block

Mainly in flats and post-war builds. Very hard — you may need a hammer drill (SDS) for anything substantial. Use masonry anchors. Drill slowly to avoid cracking.

How to Tell What You Have

  1. Knock on the wall

    Hollow thud = plasterboard stud wall. Solid thunk = masonry or dot-and-dab. Dull thud in spots = dot-and-dab (the hollow bits are between the dabs).

  2. Push a pin in a discreet spot

    If it goes straight through easily, you have plasterboard. If it hits resistance after a few mm, it's masonry or a stud.

  3. Check the age and location of your home

    External walls in UK homes are almost always solid masonry. Internal walls vary. Pre-1950s = likely lath & plaster. Post-1970s = likely plasterboard stud or dot-and-dab.

  4. Use a stud finder

    Good models detect both studs and cables/pipes. Run it slowly across the wall. It'll beep when it finds a stud or density change. Mark both edges to find the centre.

Section 03

Choosing the Right Fixing

The fixing is what actually holds your thing to the wall. Get this right and nothing comes off. Get it wrong and things fall. Here's your cheat sheet:

Wall TypeLoadFixing to UseNotes
Solid masonry Light Plastic wall plug + screw (e.g. yellow/red Rawlplug) Drill 6–8mm hole, tap plug in flush, drive screw
Solid masonry Medium Nylon frame fixing or longer masonry plug Match plug size to drill bit; use 8–10mm
Solid masonry Heavy Chemical anchor (resin fixing) or expanding bolt For shelves holding books, TVs, heavy mirrors
Plasterboard (stud wall) Light Hollow wall anchor (e.g. Gripit, Toggler) No stud needed; rated weight usually printed on pack
Plasterboard (stud wall) Medium–Heavy Screw directly into timber stud Find the stud with a finder; use 4mm wood screw 50–60mm long
Dot-and-dab plasterboard Light Hollow wall anchor (same as above) Or drill through to masonry behind and use wall plug
Dot-and-dab plasterboard Heavy Long masonry bolt through plasterboard into wall behind Use 100mm+ screw to reach the brick/block behind
Lath & plaster Light Picture hook nailed into lath, or plasterboard anchor Drill carefully — old plaster cracks easily
Concrete Heavy Hammer drill + masonry plug + screw, or chemical anchor Regular drills struggle — hire/borrow an SDS drill if needed
💡 Buying Fixings

Rawlplug, Fischer, and Gripit are the go-to UK brands found at B&Q, Screwfix, and Wickes. Packs are usually labelled by load rating (kg). When in doubt, go one size up — overspeccing a fixing costs pennies and adds peace of mind.

Section 04

What You're Hanging

Different objects have very different requirements. Here's a quick-reference breakdown:

Pictures & Artwork

For anything under 5kg, a single picture hook (the kind you hammer a nail through at an angle) is often all you need on plaster over masonry. For heavier framed pieces, use two fixings spaced to match the picture wire or D-rings on the back. Use a spirit level across both — don't eyeball it.

💡 The Paper Template Trick

Hold the frame against the wall where you want it, trace around it lightly in pencil, then measure where the hanging hardware sits relative to the frame top. Transfer those measurements to the wall. Drill with confidence.

Shelves

Shelves loaded with books or plants are one of the heaviest things most people hang. Always use two brackets minimum, fixed into studs or with heavy-duty hollow wall anchors. For a 900mm shelf, space brackets no more than 400mm from each end. Floating shelves (no visible bracket) use a French cleat or recessed rail — follow the specific product instructions, but still fix into studs where possible.

Mirrors

Heavy and often awkward. Most large mirrors come with two D-rings or a mirror plate on the back. Fix two screws at matching heights, hang the mirror, then slide it sideways so both D-rings sit on their screws. Use a spirit level before marking — a tilted mirror is very obvious. For anything over 10kg, use masonry fixings in a solid wall, not plasterboard anchors.

Curtain Poles & Blinds

These take consistent sideways and downward pull as you open and close them. Brackets must go into studs or masonry — never just plasterboard. Most poles come with wall brackets and screws; don't substitute smaller screws to be safe. Fix the end brackets first, then check everything is level before fixing the middle bracket.

Flat-Screen TVs

A 55" TV on a wall bracket is one of the riskier DIY jobs — the TV itself may only be 15kg, but the leverage on the fixings is significant. Always fix into studs (for a stud wall) or use M8 expanding masonry bolts in solid brick. Check the TV's VESA pattern (the bolt hole spacing on the back) before buying a bracket. Tilt/swivel brackets need especially solid fixing due to the extra leverage.

⚠ TV Bracket Safety

If you're not confident about the wall type or you're in a rented flat, it's genuinely worth paying a handyperson £50–80 to do this one. A fallen TV can hurt someone and cause serious damage.

Coat Hooks & Key Hooks

These seem simple but take a lot of lateral tugging. Use a backing board (a piece of timber screwed into studs, with hooks screwed to the timber) if the hooks are on a stud wall. On solid masonry, standard wall plugs and screws work well.

Clocks

Usually a single small hook or screw is fine. The only issue is getting the height right — measure up from the floor, not down from the ceiling, for consistency with other items.

Section 05

The Step-by-Step Method

Follow this sequence every time and you'll rarely go wrong.

  1. Decide position and mark it with masking tape

    Stick a small piece of masking tape where you want to drill. It stops the drill bit from skidding on smooth plaster or tiles, and it's easy to remove cleanly. Mark your centre point with a pencil on the tape.

  2. Scan for cables and pipes

    Run your cable detector over the area. Pay special attention to anything directly above a socket, switch, or light fitting. If in doubt, move your position slightly rather than risk it.

  3. Identify your wall type and choose your fixing

    Use the knock test, pin test, or stud finder as described above. Select the appropriate wall plug and screw for the load — refer to the table in Section 03.

  4. Set your drill depth

    Hold the wall plug next to the drill bit and wrap a piece of masking tape around the bit at the same depth as the plug's length. This prevents you drilling too deep. Most plugs are 25–38mm long.

  5. Drill the hole

    For masonry: set your drill to hammer mode, start slowly to create a starter hole, then increase speed. Keep the drill perpendicular to the wall — don't angle it or the plug won't sit straight. Stop when your depth tape hits the wall surface.

  6. Clear the dust

    Blow out the hole or use a vacuum. Dust left in the hole stops the plug gripping properly.

  7. Insert the wall plug

    Push it in by hand until it's flush with the wall surface. If it's slightly loose, add a tiny dab of PVA glue around the plug. If the hole is too big, use the next size up plug or pack it with toothpicks and PVA.

  8. Drive the screw

    Use the correct screwdriver bit (usually PZ2 for wall screws). Drive it in firmly but don't overtighten — the plug needs to expand and grip. Stop when the screw head is snug against the wall or fitting. Give the fixing a firm tug test before hanging anything on it.

  9. Hang and level

    For items with two fixing points, hang loosely on both, use a spirit level to get it straight, then tighten everything up. Small adjustments: tap lightly with the heel of your hand to shift things a millimetre or two.

📌 Masonry Drilling Tips

If your drill starts spinning but not cutting (you can smell burning), you've hit a piece of flint or hard aggregate in the mortar. Pull out, let it cool, and try at a slightly different spot just a few mm away. Don't force it — you'll blunt the bit or crack the plaster.

Section 06

No-Drill Options

Renting? Don't want holes? For lighter items there are good alternatives. Just be honest about the weight limits — these are not substitutes for proper fixings on heavy things.

🟦

Command Strips (3M)

Adhesive strips rated up to 7.2kg per pair on smooth, painted surfaces. Remove cleanly by pulling the tab down slowly. Won't work on textured or freshly painted walls (wait 7 days after painting).

🪝

Adhesive Hooks

Good for coats, bags, light items. Command and Tesa are reliable brands. Check the weight rating. Not suitable for anything over ~3–5kg.

🧲

Magnetic Fixings

Only useful if you have metal surfaces (some modern radiators, steel panels). Strong rare-earth magnets can hold surprisingly well for small items.

🖼️

Picture Ledges

A shelf with a lip that artworks lean against. One row of fixings, then you can swap artwork freely without making new holes. Popular option for renters.

🏗️

Freestanding Solutions

Floor-to-ceiling tension poles (like those from IKEA or Umbra) for shelving. No fixings needed. Work well in alcoves and corners.

🪴

Furniture-Mounted

Hook over a door, clamp to a shelf edge, or rest against skirting board. For things like blanket ladders, some art displays, and plant hangers.

Section 07

Common Mistakes & How to Avoid Them

MistakeWhat HappensHow to Avoid It
Wrong drill bit for wall type Masonry bit skates on wood; wood bit burns out on brick Identify the wall first; buy a mixed bit set
Drilling at an angle Plug sits crooked; screw pulls out sideways Look at drill from two angles; use a drill guide if unsure
Hole too big for plug Plug spins and won't grip Use the size up; or pack hole with matchsticks and PVA
Not clearing dust from hole Plug grips poorly; fixing fails under load Blow or vacuum the hole before inserting plug
Eyeballing level Everything looks tilted after hanging Always use a spirit level; even a phone app level is fine
Plasterboard anchor in dot-and-dab cavity Anchor expands in the air gap behind, not in the board Drill through to masonry; use a long bolt instead
Overtightening into masonry plugs Plug cracks or pulls out under the screw Stop when snug; the plug should expand, not compress
Hanging near hidden cables Possible electrocution, power outage, fire Always scan with a detector; never skip this step
Measuring from the wrong reference point Hooks don't match across the room Always measure up from the floor, not down from ceiling
✦

Quick Reference: Patching Mistakes

You will make mistakes. That's fine — walls are patchable. For small screw holes in plaster or plasterboard, use Polyfilla (a ready-mix filler from B&Q), press it in with a filling knife or even a finger, let it dry, then sand smooth and touch up with paint. For larger holes in plasterboard (up to about 50mm), use a mesh repair patch, skim over with filler, and sand once dry. Colour-matching paint is the hardest part — always keep a small pot of the wall paint when you decorate.

💡 Final Golden Rule

Measure twice, drill once. Everything in this guide comes back to that. Take an extra 60 seconds to double-check your marks before drilling, and most problems simply don't happen.
